Atlas Gimbal — a 2-axis stabilizer where PID, a learned net, and online adaptation share one loop.

№ 01 / 08
2025 — 2026
PID · stable · nominal
click to expand
mechanics · control · reinforcement learning

One rig, three layers of control. PID keeps it safe, a learned net makes it sharp, online adaptation holds the line.

A 2-axis brushless camera gimbal on a Storm32 mount, sensed by an MPU-6050 IMU at 200 Hz and run entirely on a ~380 MAD ESP32. Identified as a lightly-damped 2ⁿᵈ-order plant (ωₙ ≈ 12.4 rad/s, ζ ≈ 0.18), it pits a cascade PID against a PPO policy — then fuses them. Under added load and delay the PID falls into a self-sustained 1.9 Hz limit cycle; the autonomous controller stacks an auto-tuned PID, a frozen PPO residual net, and a safe online bias to erase that cycle (> 99 %) and keep erasing it as the hardware drifts.

Featured study · full detail

Atlas Gimbal — a 2-axis stabilizer that auto-tunes its PID, adds a frozen learned residual, and keeps adapting — all on one ESP32.

v0.6 · 2026
monomial · single bench
~ 25 pages
MP · spécialité info
1 · problem

Can a learned policy beat a hand-tuned PID on a real physical system, especially when non-linearities dominate?

Stabilization is the canonical problem of classical control. PID has ruled since the 1940s — three gains, a simple formula, formal guarantees for linear, stationary systems. But the real world is neither linear nor stationary: actuator saturation, dry friction, and communication delays all break the assumptions PID rests on. The textbook consequence is a limit cycle — a self-sustained bounded oscillation predicted by Slotine & Li (1991) via the describing-function method.

Since AlphaGo (2016), reinforcement learning has popularised a different paradigm: an agent that learns by trial and error can outperform hand-crafted heuristics. The question this study asks is direct: does that translate to physical stabilization too, when the simple control theory hits its wall?

3 · classical control

The PID baseline — then taught to tune itself

The reference controller is a PID with a 50 Hz low-pass on the derivative term (to reject IMU noise) and saturation-based anti-windup on the integrator. A fixed, hand-set gain triplet is the conservative fallback.

control law · static fallback gains u(t) = Kp · e(t) + Ki · ∫ e(τ) dτ + Kd · de/dt
safe defaults : Kp = 4.2 · Ki = 8.0 · Kd = 0.15

But one gain set never covers the whole operating range — so the ESP32 tunes itself at every startup. It excites each axis with a 0.3 step, identifies ωₙ, ζ and K by logarithmic decrement, then solves Kp, Ki, Kd by pole placement (target ζ_d = 0.8, third pole at 3·ω_d). Both axes are calibrated separately — the roll's slightly higher inertia gives it a 5 % lower pulsation. Total self-tuning time ≈ 6 s.

auto-tuned gains · per axis (pole placement) pitch : ωₙ 12.53 · ζ 0.182 · K 1.60 → Kp 3.00 · Ki 23.5 · Kd 0.211 → t_r 0.275 s · 10.1 % OS
roll  : ωₙ 11.88 · ζ 0.203 · K 1.55 → Kp 3.10 · Ki 23.0 · Kd 0.228 → t_r 0.300 s · 9.7 % OS
4 · reinforcement learning

The MDP formulation — state, action, reward

The problem is recast as a Markov Decision Process. The state is the per-axis angle, angular velocity, setpoint and error — an 8-dimensional observation across both axes. Action is the normalised command to each motor (dim 2). Reward is the negative squared pointing error plus a small penalty on commanded effort.

observation (8D) · reward s = [ θ, θ̇, ref, err ]pitch ⊕ [ θ, θ̇, ref, err ]roll
r(t) = − ‖ err(t) ‖² − α · ‖ a(t) ‖²   with α = 0.01

Why the setpoint is in the observation, not just the error. After normalisation, angle and error are rescaled separately, so the network cannot reconstruct the target by subtraction. Feeding the setpoint explicitly tells the agent both where it is and where it must go — more informative, and more general.

The agent is trained with PPO — Proximal Policy Optimization — whose clipped surrogate bounds every update, keeping training stable. ε = 0.2 is the published default and proves robust here.

PPO clipped surrogate LCLIP(θ) = Et[ min( rt(θ)·Ât , clip(rt(θ), 1−ε, 1+ε)·Ât ) ]
ε = 0.2 · γ = 0.99 · GAE λ = 0.95 · ≈ 1 M timesteps · CPU, 4 parallel envs
5 · plant identification

The gimbal as a 2nd order plant

A step of normalised torque u0 = 0.3 is sent via Storm32; angle θ(t) is logged at 200 Hz, and a second-order underdamped model is fit from the damped step response by logarithmic decrement.

identified transfer function θ(s) / u(s) = K · ωₙ² / ( s² + 2 ζ ωₙ s + ωₙ² )
ωₙ ≈ 12.4 rad/s · ζ ≈ 0.18 · K ≈ 1.6
model-vs-measurement residual = 2.3 % RMS

The clean model is deliberately re-roughened: three non-linearities are reinjected into the training simulator — torque saturation (clip ± 1), dry Coulomb friction (−μ · sign θ̇), and a pure command delay (a FIFO of length τ / dt). On top of that, domain randomization perturbs every physical parameter by ± 15 % each episode so the learned policy survives the sim-to-real jump.

Cdegraded robustness
strong friction μ = 0.08 · + 50 ms delay

Pushes the loop well past the PID's delay margin. This is where the limit cycle appears.

Each test is repeated 10 times per controller — 60 logged traces in all — and analysed statistically, with an FFT to pin the limit-cycle frequency.

Self-tuning already halves the static PID's settling time and overshoot. PPO goes further — fastest, lowest overshoot, and 2.4× more delay margin.

Test C · the 1.9 Hz limit cycle appears

Under strong friction and added delay, the static PID enters a self-sustained oscillation at f = 1.9 Hz, amplitude ± 0.08 rad. The linearised pulsation predicts fn = ωₙ / 2π = 1.97 Hz — agreement within ~ 4 %, exactly the limit cycle Slotine & Li's describing-function analysis predicts for a linear controller against a hard non-linearity. A linear loop, by construction, cannot guarantee it away. The learned controller can.

8 · the autonomous controller★ core of the project

Don't replace the PID — stack three layers on it

Replacing a PID with a raw neural network is risky: a learned policy alone offers no stability guarantee. So the design enriches the PID instead. The final command is a sum of three terms, each with a clear, complementary job — and the whole thing runs autonomously on the ESP32.

u = uPID + uresidual + biasadaptive
1
layer 1 · classicalfixed per session
Self-tuned PID

Calibrates Kp, Ki, Kd on both axes at startup by pole placement (≈ 6 s). Guarantees baseline stability — the floor everything else stands on.

2
layer 2 · learned, frozenresidual RL · ~1300 params
Frozen PPO residual net

A tiny MLP (10→32→32→2, tanh) trained offline so its output is the correction added to the PID. Exported to C++ and frozen on-device (~250 µs/eval). It can only improve, never destabilise — the PID underneath holds.

3
layer 3 · online, boundedlearns while running
Safe online adaptation

Learns just 2 scalars (one bias per axis) via a slow moving average to track wear, heating and battery sag. Three guards keep it safe: hard clip |bias| ≤ 0.15, a trust region that reverts on a > 20 % RMS rise, and a fall-back to PID-only past a critical error.

Layer 2 erases the limit cycle. In the degraded case where the PID oscillates, the frozen residual net flattens it on both axes — a reduction of more than 99 %, and reproducibly so.

pitch axis · degraded case · θ over time PID only + residual

Layer 3 stays safe where naïve online learning blows up. A naïve per-step gradient is dangerous here: the loop's delay means a command's effect only lands ~10 steps later, so the instant gradient often points the wrong way. Across 10 random seeds in the degraded case, the naïve version is ~20× worse and unpredictable — the bounded-bias scheme matches the frozen reference.

residual RMS error · degraded · 10 seeds (lower is better)rad
frozen residual (ref)0.056
safe adaptation0.059
naïve online1.10

And the bias actually does its job: on a PD loop left with a constant imbalance (no integral term), online adaptation cuts the steady-state error from 0.124 rad to 0.008 rad — a 94 % reduction, the correction converging cleanly without ever diverging. Glue a 1–2 g mass to one arm at the oral and the gimbal re-levels itself, hands-off, in seconds.

It all fits on a ≈ €35 ESP32, in real time

The whole stack — sensor read, self-tuned PID, frozen residual forward pass and the online adapter — runs inside the 5 ms budget of a 200 Hz loop, measured at the microsecond level. 1.46 ms used: 29 % of the budget, with comfortable margin.

per 5 ms control cycle (200 Hz)1.46 ms  · 29 % used

9 · discussion
why the learned layers win here
  • Non-linear by design — the residual shapes the command per state, where PID is linear by construction.
  • Optimises the future — PPO maximises discounted return, not just instantaneous error.
  • Trained on perturbation — domain randomization (± 15 %) is baked into the training distribution.
honest limits
  • Training cost — hours of CPU vs. ~20 min to tune a PID. Fine for research.
  • Partial black box — no Lyapunov proof for the net alone; but the PID floor + bounded bias + guards give practical stability.
  • Sim-to-real gap — randomization mitigates it; the online layer absorbs the rest on the bench.

The thesis in one line: the PID guarantees stability, the neural network improves performance, and online adaptation keeps that performance over time — each layer with a clear, complementary role. The key word is safe, not autonomous-at-any-cost.
